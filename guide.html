Brazil 101 · the 60-second brief

What you actually need to know

🗣️
Language
Portuguese (not Spanish). English is patchy outside hotels — Google Translate's camera is your friend.
💵
Money
The Real (R$). Roughly R$6–7 = £1. Card works almost everywhere; carry some cash for boats, tips, beach kiosks.
🌡️
Weather
It's spring there. Rio warm (25–30°C), Amazon hot & humid, Iguaçu mild. Pack light + one rain layer.
🔌
Plugs & SIM
Plug type N (bring an adapter). Grab an eSIM (Airalo) before you fly — instant data on landing.
🛡️
Safety
Big cities = street-smart rules: cheap phone out, no flashy watches, Uber after dark. The nature spots are very chill.
🚕
Getting around
Uber is cheap and everywhere in cities. Between cities = short domestic flights and a couple of boats.
💊
Before you go
Check yellow-fever vaccine (often advised for the Amazon) + travel insurance. No major visa hassle for short stays.
💬
3 magic words
Oi (hi), obrigado (thanks), cerveja (beer). You're basically fluent.
The story of this trip
You start loud and iconic in Rio, decompress on a car-free island, stand under the planet's most insane waterfalls, go fully off-grid in the Amazon, then land in the giant party-city of São Paulo to finish. It builds from city → nature → wild → and back to city. Each leg has a totally different energy on purpose.
① FIRST STOP⭐ 4.9 · Iconic
Rio de Janeiro
Southeast coast · "the Marvelous City"
Sept 17–20 · 4 nights

Beaches, mountains and music crammed into one city — the postcard Brazil everyone pictures.

Getting there
You fly straight in (airport code GIG or SDU). From the airport, an Uber to the beach neighbourhoods (Copacabana / Ipanema) takes ~40 min. You're staying in an Airbnb near the beach.

Rio is built around nature — green mountains drop straight into the sea, and the city wraps around the beaches. The famous Christ the Redeemer statue stands on a peak watching over all of it. It's lively, loud and friendly, with a strong beach-and-football culture.

Your four days mix the big sights (Christ, Sugarloaf cable car), the beaches (Copacabana & Ipanema), a real football match at the Maracanã stadium, and a steak night. It's the "wow, we're really here" opener.

Good to know
  • Book Christ the Redeemer online in advance — it's timed entry and sells out.
  • Beaches work on kiosks: you rent a chair + umbrella and waiters bring drinks to you. Tip a few reais.
  • Take a cheap phone or just cash to the beach. Don't leave stuff unattended while you swim.
  • Caipirinha = Brazil's national cocktail (lime, sugar, cachaça). Strong. Pace yourself.
What to expect
High energy and a bit of healthy big-city caution. Stunning by day, buzzing by night. You'll leave wanting more — which is perfect, because next is the opposite.
② RESET⭐ 4.8 · Paradise
Ilha Grande
Island off the coast, ~3 hrs south of Rio
Sept 21–23 · 3 nights

A car-free tropical island where the only way around is by boat or trail. Total decompress.

Getting there
Early start from Rio: a road transfer (~2.5 hrs) to the coast, then a boat to the island's main village, Abraão. There are no cars on the island — you walk everywhere or hop boats. Phone signal is patchy (that's the point).

After loud Rio, Ilha Grande is the exhale. It's a lush jungle island ringed with beaches, including Lopes Mendes — regularly voted one of Brazil's best. Days are about hiking jungle trails to hidden beaches, boat trips to snorkel in clear lagoons, and fresh seafood by the water at night.

You stay in a pousada (a small, family-run guesthouse — think cosy B&B). It's barefoot, slow, and beautiful.

Good to know
  • Pack a small day-bag for the island — leave the big stuff in Rio storage if you can. Boats + trails = travel light.
  • Bring cash: smaller places may not take cards, and ATMs are limited.
  • Reef-safe sunscreen + water shoes help a lot. The sun is strong.
  • Download offline maps/music before you arrive — signal is genuinely spotty.
What to expect
Slow, sweaty, gorgeous. No nightclubs — the "night out" is caipirinhas and grilled fish by the water. You'll sleep well.
③ JAW-DROP⭐ 5.0 · Bucket-list
Iguaçu Falls
Far south, on the Argentina/Brazil border
Sept 24–25 · 2 nights

Not "a waterfall" — 270+ waterfalls in a giant horseshoe. It makes Niagara look small.

Getting there
A travel-heavy day: off the island back toward Rio, then a 2-hour domestic flight to Foz do Iguaçu. You stay in a hotel in the town of Foz, right by the falls park.

Iguaçu is one of the great natural wonders of the world — a vast wall of waterfalls straddling the Brazil–Argentina border, surrounded by jungle. The highlight is the Devil's Throat, a U-shaped chasm where the water roars so hard it throws up permanent mist and rainbows.

The Argentine side (which you cross the border to visit) has walkways that take you right over and into the falls. You will get wet. It's worth it.

Full day on the walkways — Upper, Lower & the Devil's Throat. Bring your passport (border crossing) and budget 4–6 hrs.
Good to know
  • Passport required — you cross into Argentina for the best side. Keep it on you.
  • Wear quick-dry clothes and put your phone in a waterproof pouch/ziplock. The spray is no joke.
  • Optional adrenaline add-ons exist (speedboat under the falls, helicopter over them) — that's a quiz question.
  • Two currencies in play (Brazilian Real + Argentine Peso). Card is easiest; carry a little of each.
What to expect
Genuine "I can't believe this is real" energy. A lot of walking, a lot of mist, the best photos of the trip. Then it's onto the wildest leg.
④ THE WILD⭐ 4.9 · Once in a lifetime
Manaus & the Amazon
Deep north, in the heart of the rainforest
Sept 26–29 · 4 nights

A city in the middle of the jungle, then the jungle itself. The trip's wild card.

Getting there
No direct flights — you connect through São Paulo or Brasília (~4–5 hrs total) to Manaus, a surprisingly big city surrounded by rainforest. From there it's a 2–4 hr boat to a jungle lodge.

Manaus sits where two great rivers meet — the dark Rio Negro and the sandy Solimões — which run side by side without mixing for miles (the famous "Meeting of the Waters"). The city itself has a wild backstory: a rubber boom built a grand European-style opera house in the middle of the jungle.

The real experience is heading into the rainforest: a jungle lodge reached by boat, where you do night canoe trips spotting caimans (small crocodilians), fish for piranhas, look for pink river dolphins, and trek with local guides. Phones mostly off. Nature fully on.

Good to know
  • Yellow-fever vaccination is commonly advised for the Amazon — sort it well in advance.
  • Pack insect repellent (DEET), long sleeves, a head-torch and quick-dry layers. It's hot and humid.
  • Lodges are all-inclusive and handle every activity & transfer — you just show up.
  • Expect basic comforts (the rustic-vs-comfort lodge level is a quiz question). Limited power & wifi.
What to expect
The most "adventure" part of the trip. Hot, buggy, unforgettable. You'll come back with the best stories.
⑤ FINALE⭐ 4.7 · Big city
São Paulo
The huge economic capital, southeast
Sept 30 – Oct 4 · 4 nights

South America's biggest city — food, art and legendary nightlife. The send-off.

Getting there
Fly from Manaus to São Paulo. (Heads-up: on Sept 30 the group splits — Sathish flies home from Rio, everyone else heads to SP.) You stay at Alex's place, and fly home from GRU airport on Oct 4.

São Paulo isn't postcard-pretty like Rio — it's a sprawling concrete giant — but it's where Brazilians go for the best food and nightlife in the country. Think incredible restaurants, world-class clubs, street art, and a buzzing creative scene.

With a local (Alex) showing you around, you get the insider version: the Mercado Municipal food market, the arty Vila Madalena district, Avenida Paulista, rooftop bars, and a serious night out (the techno club D-Edge is famous). A strong finish.

Good to know
  • It's massive — use Uber and don't expect to walk between districts.
  • Clubs start very late (midnight+) and go till sunrise. Nap before going out.
  • Same street-smart rules as Rio: low-key in transit, valuables away.
  • Save room (and budget) for one blowout meal — SP is a genuine food capital.
What to expect
Urban, modern, indulgent. The "go out with a bang" chapter before the flight home.
Cheat sheet

Words you'll hear

Pousada
A small, family-run guesthouse / B&B. Common on the island.
Rodízio
All-you-can-eat — at a churrascaria, waiters bring endless skewers of meat until you tap out.
Churrascaria
A Brazilian steakhouse / barbecue restaurant.
Caipirinha
The national cocktail: lime, sugar and cachaça (sugarcane spirit). Delicious, strong.
Boteco
A casual neighbourhood bar for cold beer and small plates (petiscos).
Real (R$)
The currency. Plural "reais". ~R$6–7 to £1.
Praia
Beach. You'll see it everywhere.
Obrigado / Obrigada
Thank you (men say obrigado, women obrigada).
